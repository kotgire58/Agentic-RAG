# Commodity Markets: Supply, Demand, and Futures Trading Strategies

**Intermediate Finance Series | Commodities**

Commodity markets are unique because the underlying assets are physical goods with real supply and demand fundamentals. Unlike equities (where price is primarily a function of future earnings expectations), commodity prices are driven by tangible imbalances between production and consumption, storage constraints, seasonal patterns, and geopolitical disruptions.

## Understanding Commodity Market Fundamentals

### The Commodity Price Cycle
Commodities go through long, identifiable cycles driven by supply response lag:

1. **Low price phase:** Producers cut investment in new capacity (mines, wells, farmland)
2. **Supply tightens:** Existing capacity depreciates, demand grows
3. **Price spike:** Supply deficit causes rapid price increase
4. **Investment boom:** High prices attract massive capital investment
5. **Supply glut:** New capacity comes online simultaneously, oversupplying market
6. **Price crash:** Returns to phase 1

**Key insight:** The cure for high prices is high prices (induces new supply). The cure for low prices is low prices (kills uneconomical supply). Commodity investors profit by identifying where in the cycle a market is positioned.

### Physical vs. Financial Markets
- **Spot market:** Immediate delivery at current price
- **Futures market:** Agreed price for future delivery
- **Basis:** Difference between futures price and local cash price (reflects transportation, storage, quality differences)
- **Cash-and-carry arbitrage:** If futures price exceeds spot + carrying costs, buy physical and sell futures (risk-free profit eliminates the divergence)

## Crude Oil: The Most Important Commodity

### Market Structure
Global oil market ~100 million barrels/day demand:
- **OPEC+ production:** ~40% of global supply (Saudi Arabia, Russia, UAE, Iraq as key members)
- **U.S. shale:** ~13 million bbl/day (swing producer, responds quickly to price changes)
- **IEA members:** Strategic Petroleum Reserves (SPR) can release ~60 million barrels short-term

### WTI vs. Brent Crude
- **WTI (West Texas Intermediate):** U.S. benchmark, priced at Cushing, Oklahoma hub. CME futures symbol: CL.
- **Brent Crude:** International benchmark, North Sea oil. More representative of global seaborne trade.
- **WTI-Brent spread:** Reflects U.S. export capacity, pipeline constraints, quality differences. Typically $2-4 range; blows out during U.S. infrastructure constraints.

### Key Oil Price Drivers

**OPEC+ decisions:** Cartel managing ~40% of supply. Surprising production cuts → immediate price spike. Quota cheating by members undermines price support.

**U.S. shale response:** Shale is unique — production responds to price within 6-12 months (vs. 5-7 years for conventional oil). Above ~$65 WTI, U.S. shale producers add rigs aggressively, capping price rallies.

**Inventory data:**
- **EIA Weekly Petroleum Report (Wednesday 10:30 AM ET):** Crude oil, gasoline, distillate inventory changes. Immediate market mover.
- **Cushing stocks:** Physical crude at delivery point for WTI futures. Falling Cushing stocks = bullish.
- **Days of supply:** Inventory normalized by consumption rate — more meaningful than raw inventory numbers.

**Seasonal patterns:**
- Q2/Q3 summer driving season → gasoline demand peak
- Q4 heating oil demand (distillates)
- Refinery maintenance in spring/fall → crack spread fluctuations

**Dollar correlation:** Oil is priced in USD globally. Strong dollar makes oil more expensive for non-USD buyers, reduces demand. Inverse correlation between DXY (dollar index) and oil prices.

### Trading Crude Oil Futures (CL)
- Contract: 1,000 barrels, tick size $0.01 = $10/tick
- Initial margin: ~$5,000-7,000 per contract
- Leverage: ~20-25:1
- Average daily range: $1-3/barrel = $1,000-3,000 per contract

**Key trading levels:**
- Psychological round numbers ($70, $80, $90)
- Prior year high/low
- OPEC+ target ranges (often communicated by Saudi oil minister)
- 200-week MA for long-term trend

## Gold: The Monetary Metal

### Gold Price Determinants
Unlike industrial commodities, gold is primarily a monetary asset:

**Real interest rates (most important):** Gold pays no yield. When real rates (nominal rates minus inflation) are low or negative, gold's opportunity cost is low → gold rallies. When real rates rise sharply (2022), gold struggles despite inflation.

**DXY (Dollar strength):** Gold priced in USD. Inverse relationship — dollar weakens, gold rises in USD terms.

**Central bank buying:** Central banks (especially China, India, Turkey, Russia) have been net buyers for 10+ consecutive years. Large purchases provide structural demand floor.

**Geopolitical risk/safe haven demand:** Flight to safety during crises. Spikes on geopolitical events but typically fades as crisis subsides.

**ETF flows:** GLD, IAU, and other gold ETFs hold physical gold. Large inflows/outflows move price.

### Gold vs. Gold Mining Stocks
- **Physical gold (GLD, IAU):** Direct exposure to gold price, no operational risk
- **Senior miners (GDX ETF):** Newmont, Barrick — leveraged to gold price. If gold goes up 10%, miners may go up 20-30% (operating leverage). But add management, geopolitical, operational risk.
- **Junior miners (GDXJ ETF):** Exploration companies with high risk/reward. Some are essentially gold call options.

**Rule of thumb:** Gold miners provide ~2-3× leverage to gold price, with higher volatility.

### Silver: Industrial + Monetary Metal
- ~50-60% industrial use (solar panels, electronics, EV batteries)
- ~40-50% investment/jewelry demand
- More volatile than gold (smaller market, industrial exposure)
- **Gold/Silver ratio:** Historically 60-80:1. Extreme highs (>85) suggest silver is cheap relative to gold; extreme lows (<50) suggest expensive. Reversion trades are popular.

## Agricultural Futures: Weather, Crop Reports, and Seasonality

### The USDA WASDE Report
Monthly World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates (WASDE) is the single most important report for grain markets:
- Released ~10th of each month at noon ET
- Contains production estimates, ending stocks, global supply/demand balance
- Corn, soybeans, wheat move 2-5% immediately on surprise data
- Options traders specifically trade "WASDE straddles" — buy before, sell after

### Corn (ZC) and Soybean (ZS) Fundamentals
**U.S. is largest producer/exporter of both:**
- Corn: 30-35% of global production
- Soybeans: 30% of global production (Brazil now near-equal)

**Seasonal patterns:**
- **Planting season (April-May):** Weather uncertainty, premiums for risk
- **Pollination (July):** Most weather-sensitive period. "July corn" weather premium is real.
- **Harvest (September-October):** Harvest pressure — farmers sell, prices often weak
- **South American crop (Jan-March):** Brazil/Argentina second crop — negative for prices if large

**Export demand:**
- China's soybean import demand is single largest driver of global soy prices
- U.S. weekly export sales (Thursday morning) — above/below expectations moves markets
- Trade war risk: 2018 China tariffs crushed soybean prices 25%

### Spread Trading in Grains
Professional traders often trade spreads rather than outright positions:

**Old crop / New crop spread:**
- Buy July corn (current crop, delivery pressure), sell December corn (new crop)
- Reflects supply tightness in current crop year vs. expectations for new crop
- Reduced directional risk, captures fundamental spread relationships

**Soybean crush spread:**
- Buy soybeans, sell soybean meal + soybean oil (products)
- Reflects processing margin
- CME offers "crush" spread as single transaction

## Contango, Backwardation, and Roll Yield

### Understanding the Futures Curve
Plotting futures prices across all delivery months creates the "futures curve":

**Contango (normal for storage commodities):**
- Far-month prices > near-month prices
- Reflects storage cost + financing cost
- Crude oil normally in contango: Dec > Nov > Oct
- ETFs holding futures "pay" to roll — they sell expiring contract (cheap) and buy next month (more expensive)
- **Implication:** Long commodity ETFs underperform spot price in prolonged contango

**Backwardation (supply tightness signal):**
- Near-month prices > far-month prices
- Signal of current supply shortage that futures market doesn't expect to persist
- Roll yield is POSITIVE — sell high (spot), buy low (forward)
- Commodity indices outperform spot price when in backwardation

**Practical application:**
- Oil in steep backwardation → immediate supply shortage, potentially bullish signal
- Oil in steep contango → oversupply, storage filling, bearish fundamental backdrop
- Volatility traders use curve shape to assess whether to be long or short near-term contracts

## Commodity Correlation with Equities

### Commodities as Portfolio Diversifiers
Historically, commodities have low or negative correlation with stocks:
- During inflationary periods: Commodities rise as stocks suffer multiple compression
- During recessions: Commodities fall (demand destruction), stocks also fall (correlated)
- **Best diversification benefit:** During stagflation (1970s, 2022) — commodities outperform everything

### The Commodity-Equity Relationship
- **Energy stocks (XLE) vs. crude oil:** High correlation, but energy stocks add management risk
- **Materials stocks vs. metals:** Miners levered to commodity prices
- **Agricultural commodity prices → food inflation → CPI → Fed policy → all asset prices**

The commodity complex, while often overlooked by equity-focused investors, is the raw material input to the entire economy. Understanding commodity cycles — and particularly how they feed into inflation and monetary policy — is essential context for any serious market participant.
